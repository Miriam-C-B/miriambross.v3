Getting a University Degree

I love learning. I knew I wanted to study something pretty early on in school. I'm the first person in my family who got a university degree so I didn't have any role models as such. Still, not studying was never really an option. I never had any doubts that I belonged at a university. Which university and what subject was a different matter.

My first idea was to study biology. In Germany pupils have to choose "tracks". At most schools this means you get to choose between a language or a science track. I picked science. We all had to do math, German and a foreign language (I picked French) for our A-levels and then two more main subjects, which depended on the track you picked. Mine were chemistry and biology. I LOVED micro-biology and organic chemistry. But when I did a test provided by a university to check if you're suitable to study biology, I failed it because I was not ok with experimenting on animals. Still am not.

So then I thought I'd study psychology. For the entire last year at school I came quite convinced that was the subject for me. I took an extracurricular class at school, read a psychology magazine and even bought a text-book. That turned out to be a really good idea! I realised within the first ten pages that this was not for me. The book listed lots of experiments over the past and what people had found out. With every experiment I read about I kept thinking, "Well, duh! Of course people behaved that way!" So psychology was out.

Luckily, I woke up one morning and the solution was right there. I loved literature. Books were about people and life. If I wanted to learn about people and life, all I had to do was read. I decided to study literature and since my favourite author was (and still is) Agatha Christie, English Literature it was.

I moved to Munich, picked Comparative Literature and Sociology as my minor subjects, changed the latter a year later to French (with a focus on literature) and never looked back.

I practiced:

  • English
  • writing papers
  • translating poetry
  • standing up for myself
  • analysing poems, plays and novels

I learned:

  • a lot about people
  • that books are intertwined with the societies and times they were written in
  • that the works of Shakespeare were a collaborative effort
  • the basics about intercultural communication and to question my own assumptions
  • about postcolonial studies
  • that intelligence, knowledge, wisdom, and a good education are completely separate skills
  • that people will always judge me because everybody has prejudices
  • that I'm ruthless if I really want to get a certain result
